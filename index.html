Why does this Caribbean takeout taste so… Indian?

A look at how history can shape flavors around the world.

By Amanda Chen

Food is often a reflection of people’s identity, culture, and background. Centuries of history have shaped the varying cuisines we see today. Spices were of particular value; they determine the flavor profile of different foods and distinguish them from one another. From as early as 2,000 BC, spices such as cinnamon from Sri Lanka and cassia from China were traded along the Silk Road and reached as far west as the Arabian Peninsula and the Iranian Plateau. Eventually, the Silk Roads even expanded to Europe.

Today, with globalization and trade, there is no doubt that the flavor palettes of some cuisines have been affected by others. In this analysis, spices were analyzed from 500 recipes pulled from the Edamam Recipe API. The spices included in this analysis showed up in at least 25% of the recipes called, and the cuisines were chosen based on what was available in the API. I will say, this analysis is limited to this particular API and the recipes that were extracted. It does not reflect the full picture of spices in different cuisines.

My guess is that you reach for the cumin in your spice cabinet way more than the garam masala that might be dusting away in the back. Some spices are way more common than others. Some are very specific to one particular cuisine.

In this sample, garlic was the absolute most common spice throughout the cuisines chosen. This may not be the most surprising thing, but what was surprising, was that it showed up in every single one of the recipes analyzed. Besides it's appealing flavor, garlic also has a rich medicinal history. It was a popular prescription among ancient physicians, used to treat pulmonary ailments, fight against infection, and protect against toxins. Garlic is a resilient plant, with the ability to grow almost anywhere and be stored for a long time. Its ancient, multi-purpose use and resilience might be contributing factors to why it has stormed the culinary world.

On the other hand, five-spice powder was the only spice that showed up in only one cuisine. This Chinese spice, which dates as far back as 4,000 BC, is a blend of star anise, fennel seeds, Szechuan peppercorns, cloves, and cinnamon. Similar to garlic, five-spice is also said to have medicinal or folkloric origins, used to balance yin and yang and reflect the 5 elements. Though this spice is still mostly found in Chinese dishes, it can be found in some other cuisines. It has been well integrated into Taiwanese cooking and is included in some Vietnamese dishes.

Besides looking at what kinds of spices appear most or least often in different cuisines, I also wanted to see if there were any cuisines that were particularly similar. I did found a surprising link between two cuisines I would’ve never connected myself. From the string of small islands nestled between North and South America to one of the world's most densely populated countries, the Caribbean and India have strikingly similar flavor palettes. Among the cuisines analyzed, these two had the greatest overlap in spices.

After doing some brief research, history can explain why. Between 1838 and 1917, after slave emancipation, over 500,000 Indians were shipped to the Caribbean to work on the plantation fields under a system called indentureship. This new workforce, though not labeled as slavery, was intended to replace the African labor on these islands. And although Indian indentureship was officially abolished in 1920, Indian culture and cuisine had permanently woven itself throughout Caribbean society. Indians are now the largest ethnic group in Trinidad and with them, came the knowledge of their food and more importantly, their spices. This is why you shouldn't be surprised if your Caribbean takeout tastes deceptively similar to Indian flavors.

This analysis only reflects a tiny portion of the cuisines that exist today, and only uses a fraction of recipes from those cuisines. There is a wealth of history and tradition behind food and I would be curious to see how these trends change as more cuisines and recipes are included. In the future, I also hope to move these static visuals over to an interactive format to allow for a more flexible and exploratory approach.
